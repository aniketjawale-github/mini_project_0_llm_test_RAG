QUESTION : Is work from home allowed?
ANSWER   :
That's a very common question, and the answer is: **it depends entirely on the specific company, job role, and sometimes even the team or manager.**

Here's a breakdown of factors:

1.  **Company Policy:**
    *   **Fully Remote:** Some companies operate entirely without a physical office, and all employees work from home (or anywhere else).
    *   **Hybrid Model:** Many companies offer a blend, where employees work a few days in the office and a few days from home each week.
    *   **Flexible/Optional:** Some companies allow work from home on an as-needed or optional basis, often with manager approval.
    *   **Office-First/Mandatory In-Office:** Other companies require employees to be in the office full-time, especially for roles that require specific equipment, in-person collaboration, or client interaction.

2.  **Job Role:**
    *   **Remote-Friendly:** Roles like software development, writing, marketing, customer service (call centers), data analysis, and many consulting jobs are often well-suited for remote work.
    *   **Less Remote-Friendly:** Jobs in manufacturing, healthcare (patient-facing), retail, hospitality, lab-based research, or roles requiring hands-on physical tasks are typically not conducive to work from home.

3.  **Team/Manager Discretion:** Even within a company that generally allows WFH, a specific team or manager might have different preferences or requirements based on their projects or team dynamics.

4.  **Country/Region:** While less common now, some regions or industries might have specific regulations or cultural norms regarding remote work.

**How to find out if work from home is allowed for your situation:**

*   **Check the Job Description:** If you're applying for a job, the description will usually state if it's remote, hybrid, or in-office.
*   **Ask During the Interview Process:** It's a perfectly valid question to ask potential employers.
*   **Consult Your Company's HR Policy/Employee Handbook:** If you're already employed, this is the official source.
*   **Speak to Your Manager:** This is often the most direct way to get clarification for your specific role and team.

In summary, while work from home has become much more common and accepted since the pandemic, it's definitely not a universal standard.

QUESTION : How many leaves do employees get?
ANSWER   :
The number of leaves employees get **varies significantly** based on several factors:

1.  **Country/Region:** Labor laws regarding leave entitlements differ greatly around the world.
2.  **Company Policy:** Companies often offer more generous leave packages than the legal minimums.
3.  **Type of Leave:** There are many different categories of leave.
4.  **Employee Seniority/Tenure:** More experienced or long-term employees often accrue more leave.
5.  **Employment Status:** Full-time, part-time, contractor status can affect entitlements.
6.  **Industry:** Some industries might have specific leave considerations.
7.  **Collective Bargaining Agreements (Unions):** Union contracts can stipulate specific leave benefits.

Here's a breakdown of common types of leave and typical ranges:

**I. Statutory Leaves (often mandated by law, though specifics vary wildly):**

1.  **Annual Leave / Vacation Leave:**
    *   **Europe:** Most EU countries mandate a minimum of 4 weeks (20 working days) of paid annual leave, often more. Some countries include public holidays in this, others are separate.
    *   **UK:** Employees are entitled to 5.6 weeks (28 days) of paid annual leave, including bank holidays.
    *   **Australia:** Typically 4 weeks (20 days) of paid annual leave per year.
    *   **Canada:** Minimum of 2 weeks (10 days) per year, increasing to 3-4 weeks after several years of service, depending on the province.
    *   **USA:** No federal law mandates paid vacation. It's entirely up to the employer. Companies typically offer:
        *   **Entry-level:** 10-15 days per year.
        *   **Mid-level/Experienced:** 15-20 days per year.
        *   **Senior/Long-tenure:** 20-25+ days per year.
    *   **India:** Varies by state and industry, typically 15-20 days per year, plus casual and sick leave.

2.  **Sick Leave:**
    *   **Europe/UK/Australia/Canada:** Often a statutory entitlement, though the amount and pay vary. Some countries have long-term sick pay schemes.
    *   **USA:** No federal law mandates paid sick leave. Some states (e.g., California, New York), and many cities, do. Companies typically offer:
        *   **Paid Sick Leave:** 5-10 days per year is common.
        *   **Unpaid Sick Leave:** Often permitted for longer illnesses under FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act) for eligible employees, but it's unpaid.

3.  **Parental Leave (Maternity, Paternity, Shared):**
    *   **Europe:** Generally very generous, with many months of paid maternity leave (e.g., 6 months to 1 year+ at varying pay rates) and increasing paternity/shared parental leave provisions.
    *   **UK:** Up to 52 weeks of maternity leave (39 weeks paid) and 2 weeks of paid paternity leave, plus shared parental leave options.
    *   **USA:** FMLA provides up to 12 weeks of *unpaid*, job-protected leave for parental care (among other things) for eligible employees. Some states and companies offer paid parental leave, but it's not federal.
    *   **Canada:** Up to 15 weeks of paid maternity benefits and up to 40 weeks of parental benefits (shared).
    *   **India:** 26 weeks of paid maternity leave.

**II. Other Common Leaves (often company policy, sometimes statutory):**

1.  **Public Holidays / Bank Holidays:**
    *   **USA:** Typically 7-10 paid public holidays per year (e.g., New Year's Day, Memorial Day, Independence Day, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas).
    *   **Europe/UK/Australia/Canada:** Often 8-12 paid public holidays per year, separate from annual leave.
2.  **Bereavement Leave (Funeral Leave):** Usually 3-5 days for immediate family members, often paid.
3.  **Jury Duty / Civic Duty Leave:** Often paid for the duration of the duty.
4.  **Personal Leave:** Some companies offer a few days for personal matters that don't fit into sick or vacation leave.
5.  **Sabbatical Leave:** Often offered by companies to long-serving employees for extended periods (e.g., 3-6 months) for professional development or personal projects, sometimes paid, sometimes unpaid.
6.  **Study Leave / Professional Development Leave:** For attending courses, conferences, etc.
7.  **Military Leave:** For employees serving in the reserves or national guard.

**How to find out for a specific employee:**

To know the exact number of leaves an employee gets, you would need to check:

*   **Their Employment Contract or Offer Letter**
*   **The Company's HR Policy Manual or Employee Handbook**
*   **Local Labor Laws**
